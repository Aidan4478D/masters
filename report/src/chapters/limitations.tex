\section{Limitations}

Despite the success of the final YOLOv11s pipeline, several limitations must be 
acknowledged regarding the data, the modeling approach, and the downstream application 
of the system.

\textbf{Imaging and Dataset Constraints}

First, the $352 \times 240$ resolution of the 511NY and NYSDOT camera feeds places
a clear limit on how much visual detail the detector can use. Many vehicles appear 
too small, too blurry, or too occluded to classify confidently, which is reflected
in the dataset by the large number of \verb|unknown_vehicle| annotations. This low 
resolution is helpful from a privacy standpoint, since it makes license plates and 
faces effectively unreadable, but it also makes the system less reliable for smaller
or farther vehicles. In addition, the camera imagery itself is not always consistent.
As shown in Figure~\ref{fig:camera-no-feed}, some cameras occasionally produce degraded 
frames due to servicing, feed glitches, motion blur, or strong lighting effects. 
Nighttime glare (Figure~\ref{fig:camera-artifact-a}), 
direct sunlight striking (Figure~\ref{fig:camera-artifact-b}), 
and image corruption (Figure~\ref{fig:camera-artifact-c})
can all reduce visibility and make vehicle boundaries harder to distinguish. These 
imaging inconsistencies introduce an additional source of noise that cannot be fully 
controlled during training.

Furthermore, although the training set contains 1,200 unique images, it was drawn 
from only 12 camera intersections, with more than half located in Manhattan and 
fewer examples from other boroughs such as the Bronx and Queens. In addition, because 
these are Pan-Tilt-Zoom (PTZ) cameras, their viewpoints are not fixed, so the dataset 
does not represent every viewing angle that may occur in practice. This could help 
explain why performance dropped on the unseen-camera benchmark, as the model learned
the original camera set well, but generalized less reliably to new views. Class 
imbalance was another important limitation. Some categories, especially \verb|motorcycle|, 
were represented by very few examples in training. With only 49 labeled motorcycles, 
that class had the weakest test-set performance and was often visually confused with 
bicycles or other small two-wheeled objects.

\textbf{Modeling and Taxonomy}

The 6-class taxonomy was designed to support MOVES-based emissions analysis, but
it is still a simplified representation of the full vehicle categories used in MOVES. 
The pipeline cannot recover the entire MOVES source-type hierarchy from imagery alone.
More detailed distinctions, such as separating a transit bus from a private coach bus
or distinguishing different truck types by use or weight class, depend on information 
that is not visible in a single low-resolution frame \cite{USEPA_road_pop_2020}.

Additionally, the current pipeline does not capture the dynamic vehicle activity 
needed for full emissions modeling. Accurate MOVES-based emissions estimates depend
not only on vehicle type, but also on operational variables such as speed and vehicle 
miles traveled (VMT) \cite{us_epa_moves3_2020}. 
Because this system processes isolated still frames, it 
cannot directly observe vehicle kinematics, stop-and-go behavior, or acceleration 
patterns. Although some of these quantities could be approximated using roadway 
speed limits or segment lengths, true operating conditions cannot be recovered from
static snapshots alone.

In addition, the detector was evaluated only with spatial bounding-box metrics such
as AP50 and mAP on still images. These metrics measure detection and classification 
quality within individual frames, but they do not capture temporal consistency or 
full video-level understanding. More advanced models, like foundation large 
vision-language models, may be able to reason about some ambiguous cases more 
flexibly, but they are not practical for this deployment setting. Running a large 
commercial model across hundreds of traffic cameras at frequent intervals would 
introduce substantial cost and latency. For that reason, efficient detectors such
as YOLO remain the more realistic choice for scalable municipal monitoring, even 
if that comes with some loss of classification detail.

\textbf{Downstream Counting and Deployment}

A major limitation of the proposed system is that it operates on isolated still 
frames rather than continuous video. The current pipeline does not track vehicles
across frames or assign persistent IDs over time, so it cannot determine whether 
a vehicle seen in one image is the same vehicle seen again later. It also cannot 
recover trajectories for vehicles that are briefly hidden by larger vehicles, 
surrounding infrastructure, or other occlusions. This limits the system’s ability 
to measure true vehicle flow in the same way as a continuous traffic sensor such 
as an Automated Traffic Recorder (ATR).

The two-minute sampling interval also creates an additional mismatch between 
detections and real traffic counts. Vehicles that pass through the scene between 
snapshots are missed entirely, which helps explain the strong undercounting observed
at faster-moving corridors such as Pelham Parkway. At the same time, vehicles that 
remain visible across multiple frames, such as parked cars, queued vehicles, or 
idling delivery trucks, may be counted more than once. As a result, the current 
approach is better suited to capturing broad traffic patterns than to recovering 
exact continuous vehicle volumes.

% ------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Overall, these limitations show that the current system is better suited to estimating 
vehicle composition in individual snapshots than to directly producing exact traffic 
volumes or full MOVES-ready emissions inputs. While the detector performs well as a 
frame-level classification tool, extending it to continuous counting and end-to-end 
emissions estimation would require temporal tracking, count calibration, and additional 
operational data such as vehicle speed. The present pipeline should therefore be viewed
as a practical first step toward camera-based traffic emissions monitoring, rather than 
a complete replacement for ATR infrastructure or full emissions modeling by itself.
