\section{Background and Related Work}

\subsection{MOtor Vehicle Emission Simulator (MOVES)}

Accurately estimating the environmental impacts of transportation requires 
quantifying vehicle activity into pollutant emissions. In the United States, 
the primary tool for this purpose is the MOtor Vehicle Emission Simulator (MOVES),
developed and maintained by the U.S. Environmental Protection Agency (EPA). MOVES
is a comprehensive emissions modeling framework used by government agencies and 
researchers to estimate emissions from mobile sources at local, state, and regional
scales \cite{USEPA_tech_2024} \cite{USEPA_overview_2025}. 
According to the MOVES Technical Guidance, the program is used by state governments
to: ``develop emission inventories for the development of state implementation 
plans (SIPs), transportation conformity determinations, and analyses required 
under the National Environmental Policy Act (NEPA)'' \cite{USEPA_tech_2024}.

MOVES operates by calculating baseline emission rates, or coefficients, for various 
vehicle classes under specific operating conditions. To compute these coefficients,
the model requires detailed inputs, including vehicle fleet composition, age 
distributions, fuel properties, weather conditions, and roadway activity data \cite{USEPA_tech_2024}.
By scaling these calculated emission coefficients with localized traffic data 
such as vehicle miles traveled (VMT) and traffic speeds, planners can conduct 
air quality assessments \cite{NY911_hill_ghg} \cite{california_air_resources_board_brake} \cite{octc_final_2021}, 
evaluate congestion mitigation strategies \cite{TAYARANI2020102620}, 
and model the environmental impact
of infrastructure changes \cite{ZHANG2013307}.

A critical requirement for emissions modeling within MOVES is accurate data on 
vehicle class distributions. The model relies on distinguishing between multiple 
vehicle types, as each has vastly different emission characteristics 
\cite{USEPA_tech_2024}. 
See Table~\ref{tab:moves-hpms} for the vehicle types trakced by MOVES. 
Currently, transportation agencies rely on traditional traffic monitoring methods 
to provide these class counts (See Section~\ref{subsec:traditional-vehicle-monitoring}. 
However, these methods typically produce sparse or 
short-duration measurements \cite{FHWA_traffic_2010}. 
This spatial and temporal limitation prevents the ability to capture the complete 
distribution of urban traffic at any point in the year, making it difficult to 
determine localized emissions coefficients.

The approach proposed in this thesis aims to bridge this data gap. By applying 
computer vision techniques to existing public traffic camera networks, the proposed
pipeline detects and classifies vehicles to generate continuous, high-resolution 
traffic composition data. Replacing short-term samples with long-term observations
allows for a much more accurate calculation of vehicle emissions coefficients. 
Ultimately, this methodology is built to support the MOVES framework, bridging 
the gap between static monitoring practices and the dynamic reality of urban traffic.

To understand the necessity of this image-based measurement framework, it is 
important to examine the current standards and limitations of urban traffic data collection.

\subsection{NYC Traffic Monitoring}

\subsubsection{Traditional Vehicle Monitoring Methods}
\label{subsec:traditional-vehicle-monitoring}

Historically, traffic monitoring in New York City has relied on a combination of 
manual observation and automated counts. For intersection and signal studies, 
NYC DOT states that inspectors conduct manual counts of vehicles and pedestrians, 
typically during morning and evening rush hours, and that these counts may include 
turning movements, school-hour activity, and off-peak periods
\cite{NYCDOT_traffic_signals}.

The NYC DOT also collects roadway traffic volume data using Automatic Traffic 
Recorders (ATRs), which measure hourly vehicle counts over several weekdays or 
weekend periods \cite{NYCDOT_traffic_signals} 
These devices are typically deployed during short-duration 
monitoring campaigns and are used to estimate traffic conditions such as peak-hour 
volumes and average daily traffic. Although NYC DOT does not publicly specify 
the exact sensor types used for each traffic counting campaign, the Federal 
Highway Administration (FHWA) identifies several technologies commonly used 
in ATR systems depending on the duration of monitoring and installation constraints
\cite{FHWA_traffic_2010}.

Long-term ATR sensors are primarily inductive-loop detectors embedded in the 
roadway surface. Inductive loops consist of wire coils installed beneath the 
pavement that generate a magnetic field. When a metal vehicle passes over the 
loop, the vehicle alters the inductance of the loop, allowing the system to 
detect and count vehicles. Because these sensors are permanently installed and 
relatively durable, they are commonly used for long-term monitoring stations on 
highways and major arterial roads
\cite{FHWA_traffic_2010} \cite{wikipedia_induction_2025}.

For short-term traffic counting, which could last from several hours to several 
weeks, transportation agencies use temporary sensors that can be installed 
quickly and removed after the counting period. According to the FHWA guidelines,
these sensors include pneumatic road tubes, piezoelectric strips, tape switches,
and temporary inductive loop detectors placed on or within the roadway
\cite{FHWA_traffic_2010}.

The most widely used temporary ATR sensor is the pneumatic road tube. Pneumatic 
tubes are flexible rubber hoses stretched across the roadway and connected to a 
roadside data recorder. When a vehicle axle passes over the tube, it compresses 
the tube and generates a pulse of air that travels through the hose to the
recorder, which logs the event as a vehicle detection. Using the timing and 
spacing between pulses, traffic counters can estimate vehicle counts, speed, 
and sometimes vehicle classification. Pneumatic tube counters are popular because 
they are inexpensive, portable, and easy to deploy for short-term traffic studies.
However, the tubes degrade over time due to repeated vehicle loading, their speed
measurements become less reliable at very low speeds (typically below 10 miles 
per hour), and their accuracy decreases when monitoring multiple lanes 
simultaneously \cite{FHWA_traffic_2010} \cite{FHWA_chapter_2014}.

The NYC DOT publishes traffic volume counts on the NYC Open Data portal collected 
by ATRs. The data has been collected since 2007 and includes vehicle counts from 
more than 2,300 distinct streets across the five boroughs \cite{NYC_open_data_atr}. 
These records provide valuable insight into traffic volumes across the city and 
are widely used for transportation planning and infrastructure analysis
\cite{NYC_mayor_ceqr_2025}.

However, these measurements represent sample counts rather than continuous monitoring.
The dataset documentation notes that the number of days recorded varies by location
and year, and many locations are only measured during short-duration campaigns 
\cite{NYC_open_data_atr} \cite{NYCMTC_hub_2026}.
In a city as spatially and temporally dynamic as New York, where traffic conditions
vary significantly by location, season, and time of day, these sampling approaches 
limit the temporal resolution of available traffic data.

NYC's regional transportation reporting reflects these characteristics of traditional
monitoring systems. The New York Metropolitan Transportation Council's Hub Bound 
Travel program, for example, measures travel entering and leaving the Manhattan 
Central Business District (defined as Manhattan south of 60th Street). The 2024 
Hub Bound report indicates that the analysis relies on multiple data sources, 
including NYCDOT tube counts at locations such as 60th Street and the East River
bridges, as well as toll transaction data from MTA Bridges and Tunnels and the 
Port Authority. The data they use is collected on a single weekday in the fall 
(2024 analysis used observations from October 16, 2024) to estimate travel 
patterns entering the CBD \cite{NYCMTC_hub_2026}. 
While these datasets provide insights into 
regional travel behavior, they still depend on periodic counts and aggregated 
agency data rather than continuous direct observation of traffic flows.

\subsubsection{Traffic-Camera Based Monitoring}

In recent years, New York City has begun exploring camera based approaches to 
street monitoring that can capture traffic activity continuously and across more 
locations. In 2023, NYC DOT launched a pilot program with the analytics company 
Viva to use ``street activity sensors'' developed by Viva to analyze street activity
\cite{NYCDOT_viva_colab} \cite{viva_street_monitor}. 
The goal of the program was to analyze the effectiveness and safety of 
programs and street designs, prioritize projects for areas most in need of safety
and other street improvements, and better understand how people are using the 
streets. The system also counts modes of travel in real-time, including cars, 
trucks, bicycles, scooters, buses, and pedestrians \cite{NYCDOT_viva_colab}. 
This approach offers the potential to expand traffic monitoring beyond the 
spatial and temporal constraints of traditional counting campaigns.

Research efforts have further demonstrated the potential of using existing traffic 
camera infrastructure as a large-scale sensing network. The NYU C2SMARTER Manhattan
Congestion Tracker, for instance, analyzes imagery from roughly 400 of the city's 
more than 900 public traffic cameras to estimate traffic density within and around
the congestion pricing zone. Images are captured approximately every 30 minutes and
processed to estimate activity for multiple road users, including cars, trucks, 
pedestrians, and bicycles \cite{NYU_tracker_2025}. 
Similarly, recent research by Turkcan et al. proposes a computer-vision pipeline 
that processes imagery from over 900 traffic cameras across New York City to analyze 
traffic conditions associated with congestion pricing policies \cite{turkcan2026visionbasedanalysiscongestionpricing}. 
These studies demonstrate how existing camera infrastructure can be leveraged to 
support continuous, large-scale traffic monitoring using computer vision.

This context motivates the approach presented in this thesis. While traditional 
methods such as manual counts and ATR-based sampling remain essential components 
of transportation monitoring programs, they are inherently limited in spatial 
coverage, duration, and labor efficiency. Camera-based systems offer the potential 
for continuous, scalable, and multimodal traffic observation using infrastructure
that is already deployed across the city. Building on this transition, this thesis 
proposes a computer-vision pipeline that uses New York City traffic camera imagery 
to detect and classify vehicles for downstream emissions estimation. In this sense,
the proposed system extends traditional traffic monitoring practices by transforming
periodic counting methods into an image-based measurement framework capable of 
supporting both transportation analysis and environmental assessment.
