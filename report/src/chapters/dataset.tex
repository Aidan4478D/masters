\section{Dataset}

To train and evaluate the vehicle detection pipeline, a custom dataset of real-world 
NYC traffic  was required. Rather than relying on pre-existing generic vehicle 
datasets (ex. Stanford Cars \cite{Krause2013CollectingAL} or BIT-Vehicle\cite{dong_vehicle_2015}), 
this study utilized publicly available traffic camera closed-circuit television 
(CCTV) feeds. This is because the physical camera infrastructure is already installed  
on hundreds of streets across the five boroughs, eliminating the need for new 
equipment and installations. Additionally, images collected from the NYC CCTV 
traffic cameras have a unique resolution and viewing angles, making it difficult 
to generalize vehicles from other datasets to vehicles from NYC CCTV images. 
Furthermore, recent state legislation authorized the New York City Department 
of Transportation (NYC DOT) to quadruple its automated camera network, expanding 
to 600 equipped intersections by the end of 2026 \cite{office_of_the_governor_2024}. 
By tapping into this existing and continually expanding network, 
this methodology ensures that the proposed vehicle detection pipeline can be 
deployed and scaled citywide with zero additional physical infrastructure work.

\subsection{Data Source}

The primary source of images was the 511NY portal, an official service operated 
in conjunction with the New York State Department of Transportation (NYSDOT), 
the New York City Department of Transportation (NYC DOT), and private video 
distribution networks such as Skyline
\cite{NYCDOT_data_feeds} \cite{NYSDOT_c038145_2025} \cite{511ny_about_rideshare}.
The 511NY system provides real-time traffic and transit information, including 
access to hundreds of live traffic cameras deployed across the five boroughs
\cite{NYCDOT_realtime}.
According to NYSDOT engineering specifications, the primary purpose of these CCTV 
cameras is to provide a means to remotely monitor traffic flow on specific sections of roadway.
By utilizing these live feeds, transportation agencies can actively detect incidents, 
analyze urban congestion, and broadcast real-time travel conditions to the public
\cite{NYCDOT_item_2024}.

The data can be accessed online through an online GUI or through the 511NY 
Developer REST API. Developers with access to this API can query various 
transportation endpoints, including 
\verb|GetCameras|, \verb|GetEvents|, and \verb|GetMessageSigns| \cite{511ny_api_docs}. 
The \verb|GetCameras| endpoint was primarily queried as it returned metadata for a 
camera's unique ID, exact coordinates (latitude and longitude), the roadway name,
and a URL to fetch the current camera image.

However, querying this endpoint only returned cameras operated by NYSDOT and 
Skyline rather than the complete city network. This limitation exists because 
state and city agencies share data differently. While NYSDOT camera feeds are 
freely accessible through the 511NY API, the New York City Department of 
Transportation (NYC DOT) requires developers to sign a formal data-sharing 
agreement to access its network of over 900 municipal cameras
\cite{NYCDOT_data_feeds} \cite{NYCDOT_realtime_metadata_2010}.
To ensure this research remains easily reproducible without legal barriers, 
the dataset was built exclusively using the unrestricted NYSDOT and Skyline feeds.

While these cameras are capable of high-resolution video, the images returned

downsampled and compressed, as this reduces server bandwidth and inherently 
enforces privacy by making it impossible to read license plates or use facial 
recognition. Thus the images used in this dataset all have the dimensions 352x240.

\subsection{Dataset Curation}

To ensure the model learned a robust representation of New York City traffic,
the data collection targeted a diverse set of intersections from different boroughs 
and types of roadways (narrow commercial streets, major expressways, and neighborhood
intersections).

These cameras were chosen based on three criteria: API availability, roadway typology, 
and location. First, regarding availability, requesting images from certain cameras

is being serviced'' (Figure~\ref{fig:camera-serviced}) or ``no live video camera

Additionally, cameras were selected at street-level intersections rather than 
highways because intersections have accelerating, braking, and idling vehicles
which produce higher emissions than steady highway cruising 
\cite{us_epa_moves3_2020}. High-resolution
studies further demonstrate that emissions vary sharply across short distances, 
with hotspots frequently forming at congested intersections which is often missed
by imprecise emission estimates \cite{wang_high-resolution_2025}. 
Also, NYC’s truck route regulations concentrate
large through-trucks on designated expressways \cite{NYCDOT_truck_routing}, 
while local street networks
are characterized by last-mile delivery vehicles, city transit buses, and other
urban service fleets \cite{NYCDOT_truck_plan_2021}. 
As a result, intersections better capture the vehicle 
composition and operating conditions that drive neighborhood-level emissions, 
which is the scale of interest in this study. Thus cameras were chosen in 
different neighborhoods in Manhattan, the Bronx, Queens, and Brooklyn. No cameras
were chosen in Staten Island as they either returned images shown in 
Figure~\ref{fig:camera-down} or they were feeds of highways.


Twelve cameras that fit these criteria were then chosen based on the viewing 
angle of the vehicle (ex. Front view, rear view, side view, and any
combination of each). This is because when developing a model to be deployed 
on any NYC traffic camera, it is necessary to make sure all views of different 
vehicle orientations are captured so the model is robust to any view of the vehicle.
Figure~\ref{fig:vehicle-views} shows four different views of vehicle orientations the cameras can 
record. The images are colored such that vehicles highlighted in yellow show 
its front, vehicles highlighted in red show its rear, and vehicles highlighted

(Figures~\ref{fig:side-rear-view} and \ref{fig:rear-front-view}) or three 
(Figure~\ref{fig:side-front-rear-view}) different vehicle orientations. The twelve

\subsubsection{Image Collection}

To automate the collection of the camera images, a Python script was developed to
save the 12 selected camera images from the 511NY API directly. Although the API 
allows up to 10 requests per minute (once every six seconds) \cite{511ny_api_docs},
and the physical 
cameras refresh their static feeds approximately every 15 seconds 
\cite{NYCDOT_realtime_subscribers}, a sampling rate
of two minutes was chosen. This was selected to align with New York City's traffic
signal cycles, which typically range from 45 to 120 seconds depending on local 
traffic volumes and intersection patterns. By capturing frames every two minutes,
the collection protocol inherently acts as a temporal filter, increasing the 
likelihood that consecutive images capture distinct vehicles rather than oversampling
the same traffic scene.

Data was collected on October 1st (Wednesday), 16th (Thursday), 20th (Monday), and 
November 3rd (Monday). Figure~\ref{fig:vehicles-hour-chart} shows the distribution
of the counts of images in the dataset by hour of the day. It shows that all images 
were taken from 9 AM to 12 AM, with the majority of images in the early afternoon, 
and during the afternoon peak traffic window (3 PM to 6 PM).

\subsection{Data Labeling}

After image collection, all frames were manually annotated to create the ground-truth
dataset used for model training and evaluation. Because the images were collected 
from low-resolution traffic CCTV feeds with varying viewpoints, weather conditions, 
and scene complexity, the labeling process required a tool that allowed precise 
bounding-box annotation and consistent class management across a large image set. 
For this purpose, the Computer Vision Annotation Tool (CVAT) was used.

CVAT is a web-based annotation platform designed for computer vision datasets. 
It supports object detection tasks through rectangular bounding boxes and allows 
custom class definitions, attribute assignment, and export to multiple machine-learning 
formats \cite{CVAT_home}. 
In this study, CVAT was used to draw a bounding box around each visible vehicle 
and assign it a class label. Additional boolean attributes were attached when a 
vehicle belonged to a more specific subtype of interest, such as a taxi, police
vehicle, minivan, or ambulance. This workflow allowed the dataset to preserve both
a core detection taxonomy and more detailed contextual information without splitting 
up the main class structure.

\subsubsection{Labeling Class Structure}

The vehicle classes designed to balance two needs: visual distinguishability in 
low-resolution CCTV imagery and relevance to downstream emissions estimation. 
Thus, in total, eight classes were used:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \verb|sedan|
    \item \verb|suv|
    \item \verb|pickup_truck|
    \item \verb|van|
    \item \verb|bus|
    \item \verb|truck|
    \item \verb|two_wheeler|
    \item \verb|unknown_vehicle|
\end{itemize}

These labels were chosen because they represent vehicle groupings that are reasonably 
aligned with broader transportation and emissions categories while still remaining 
visually identifiable in 352×240 traffic-camera images. In particular, sedans, coupes,
and hatchbacks were all labeled as \verb|Sedan|, while sport utility vehicles and crossovers 
were both labeled as \verb|SUV|. This consolidation was intentional. Federal transportation 
and emissions frameworks generally operate on broader vehicle classes rather than 
highly specific consumer body styles. For example, the FHWA Traffic Monitoring 
Guide groups sedans and coupes within the passenger-car category, while EPA and 
DOE materials similarly distinguish broad classes such as passenger cars, passenger 
trucks, buses, trucks, and motorcycles for transportation and emissions analysis 
\cite{FHWA_traffic_2014} \cite{USEPA_def_truck_2019} \cite{USDOE_AFDC}.

This simplification was also necessary because the dataset consists of low-resolution 
CCTV images, where fine-grained distinctions between similar passenger body styles 
are often visually unreliable. In many views, a coupe, sedan, and hatchback differ
primarily in roofline or rear profile, while a crossover and SUV may differ only 
subtly in ride height or body proportions. In compressed roadside imagery, these 
features are frequently obscured by distance, motion blur, occlusion, and unfavorable
camera angle. Grouping visually similar body styles into broader categories therefore 
reduced annotation ambiguity and helped limit label noise. This is especially 
important in a manually labeled dataset, where forcing uncertain distinctions can 
degrade downstream model performance.

At the same time, the annotation classes was intentionally made slightly more 
specific than the final MOVES mapping. Although SUVs and pickup trucks are later 
grouped into the MOVES passenger-truck category, they were annotated separately 
during dataset creation so that the model could first learn more specific visual 
representations of distinct vehicle forms. EPA’s MOVES framework distinguishes 
passenger cars from passenger trucks and light commercial trucks as separate 
source-use types, and EPA explicitly notes that many SUVs and minivans are considered
light-duty trucks while visual appearance alone is often insufficient to determine 
the exact analytical classification \cite{USEPA_def_truck_2019}, \cite{USEPA_road_pop_2020}. 
Separating \verb|suv| and \verb|pickup_truck|at 
the image-labeling stage therefore helped the model learn more discriminative 
visual features before later collapsing detections into broader MOVES-compatible
categories for emissions estimation.
