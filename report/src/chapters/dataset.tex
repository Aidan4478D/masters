\section{Dataset}

To train and evaluate the vehicle detection pipeline, a custom dataset of real-world 
NYC traffic  was required. Rather than relying on pre-existing generic vehicle 
datasets (ex. Stanford Cars \cite{Krause2013CollectingAL} or BIT-Vehicle\cite{dong_vehicle_2015}), 
this study utilized publicly available traffic camera closed-circuit television 
(CCTV) feeds. This is because the physical camera infrastructure is already installed  
on hundreds of streets across the five boroughs, eliminating the need for new 
equipment and installations. Additionally, images collected from the NYC CCTV 
traffic cameras have a unique resolution and viewing angles, making it difficult 
to generalize vehicles from other datasets to vehicles from NYC CCTV images. 
Furthermore, recent state legislation authorized the New York City Department 
of Transportation (NYC DOT) to quadruple its automated camera network, expanding 
to 600 equipped intersections by the end of 2026 \cite{office_of_the_governor_2024}. 
By tapping into this existing and continually expanding network, 
this methodology ensures that the proposed vehicle detection pipeline can be 
deployed and scaled citywide with zero additional physical infrastructure work.

\subsection{Data Source}

The primary source of images was the 511NY portal, an official service operated 
in conjunction with the New York State Department of Transportation (NYSDOT), 
the New York City Department of Transportation (NYC DOT), and private video 
distribution networks such as Skyline
\cite{NYCDOT_data_feeds} \cite{NYSDOT_c038145_2025} \cite{511ny_about_rideshare}.
The 511NY system provides real-time traffic and transit information, including 
access to hundreds of live traffic cameras deployed across the five boroughs
\cite{NYCDOT_realtime}.
According to NYSDOT engineering specifications, the primary purpose of these CCTV 
cameras is to provide a means to remotely monitor traffic flow on specific sections of roadway.
By utilizing these live feeds, transportation agencies can actively detect incidents, 
analyze urban congestion, and broadcast real-time travel conditions to the public
\cite{NYCDOT_item_2024}.

The data can be accessed online through an online GUI or through the 511NY 
Developer REST API. Developers with access to this API can query various 
transportation endpoints, including 
\verb|GetCameras|, \verb|GetEvents|, and \verb|GetMessageSigns| \cite{511ny_api_docs}. 
The \verb|GetCameras| endpoint was primarily queried as it returned metadata for a 
camera's unique ID, exact coordinates (latitude and longitude), the roadway name,
and a URL to fetch the current camera image.

However, querying this endpoint only returned cameras operated by NYSDOT and 
Skyline rather than the complete city network. This limitation exists because 
state and city agencies share data differently. While NYSDOT camera feeds are 
freely accessible through the 511NY API, the New York City Department of 
Transportation (NYC DOT) requires developers to sign a formal data-sharing 
agreement to access its network of over 900 municipal cameras
\cite{NYCDOT_data_feeds} \cite{NYCDOT_realtime_metadata_2010}.
To ensure this research remains easily reproducible without legal barriers, 
the dataset was built exclusively using the unrestricted NYSDOT and Skyline feeds.

\subsection{Dataset Curation}

To ensure the model learned a robust representation of New York City traffic,
the data collection targeted a diverse set of intersections from different boroughs 
and types of roadways (narrow commercial streets, major expressways, and neighborhood
intersections).

is being serviced'' (Figure~\ref{fig:camera-serviced}) or ``no live video camera

Additionally, cameras were selected at street-level intersections rather than 
highways because intersections have accelerating, braking, and idling vehicles
which produce higher emissions than steady highway cruising 
\cite{us_epa_moves3_2020}. High-resolution
studies further demonstrate that emissions vary sharply across short distances, 
with hotspots frequently forming at congested intersections which is often missed
by imprecise emission estimates \cite{wang_high-resolution_2025}. 
Also, NYC’s truck route regulations concentrate
large through-trucks on designated expressways \cite{NYCDOT_truck_routing}, 
while local street networks
are characterized by last-mile delivery vehicles, city transit buses, and other
urban service fleets \cite{NYCDOT_truck_plan_2021}. 
As a result, intersections better capture the vehicle 
composition and operating conditions that drive neighborhood-level emissions, 
which is the scale of interest in this study. Thus cameras were chosen in 
different neighborhoods in Manhattan, the Bronx, Queens, and Brooklyn. No cameras
were chosen in Staten Island as they either returned images shown in 
Figure~\ref{fig:camera-down} or they were feeds of highways.


Twelve cameras that fit these criteria were then chosen based on the viewing 
angle of the vehicle (ex. Front view, rear view, side view, and any
combination of each). This is because when developing a model to be deployed 
on any NYC traffic camera, it is necessary to make sure all views of different 
vehicle orientations are captured so the model is robust to any view of the vehicle.

\subsubsection{Image Collection}

To automate the collection of the camera images, a Python script was developed to
save the 12 selected camera images from the 511NY API directly. Although the API 
allows up to 10 requests per minute (once every six seconds) \cite{511ny_api_docs},
and the physical 
cameras refresh their static feeds approximately every 15 seconds 
\cite{NYCDOT_realtime_subscribers}, a sampling rate
of two minutes was chosen. This was selected to align with New York City's traffic
signal cycles, which typically range from 45 to 120 seconds depending on local 
traffic volumes and intersection patterns. By capturing frames every two minutes,
the collection protocol inherently acts as a temporal filter, increasing the 
likelihood that consecutive images capture distinct vehicles rather than oversampling
the same traffic scene.

Data was collected on October 1st (Wednesday), 16th (Thursday), 20th (Monday), and 
November 3rd (Monday). Figure~\ref{fig:vehicles-hour-chart} shows the distribution
of the counts of images in the dataset by hour of the day. It shows that all images 
were taken from 9 AM to 12 AM, with the majority of images in the early afternoon, 
and during the afternoon peak traffic window (3 PM to 6 PM).

\subsection{Data Labeling}

After image collection, all frames were manually annotated to create the ground-truth
dataset used for model training and evaluation. Because the images were collected 
from low-resolution traffic CCTV feeds with varying viewpoints, weather conditions, 
and scene complexity, the labeling process required a tool that allowed precise 
bounding-box annotation and consistent class management across a large image set. 
For this purpose, the Computer Vision Annotation Tool (CVAT) was used.

\begin{figure}[!htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[height=7cm]{figures/dataset/3_4_cvat_intro.png}
    \caption{CVAT environment used to label images.}
    \label{fig:cvat-labeing}
\end{figure}

\FloatBarrier

CVAT is a web-based annotation platform designed for computer vision datasets. 
It supports object detection tasks through rectangular bounding boxes and allows 
custom class definitions, attribute assignment, and export to multiple machine-learning 
formats \cite{CVAT_home}. 
In this study, CVAT was used to draw a bounding box around each visible vehicle 
and assign it a class label. Additional boolean attributes were attached when a 
vehicle belonged to a more specific subtype of interest, such as a taxi, police
vehicle, minivan, or ambulance. This workflow allowed the dataset to preserve both
a core detection taxonomy and more detailed contextual information without splitting 
up the main class structure.

\subsubsection{Labeling Class Structure}
\label{sec:label-classes}

The vehicle classes designed to balance two needs: visual distinguishability in 
low-resolution CCTV imagery and relevance to downstream emissions estimation. 
Thus, in total, eight classes were used:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \verb|sedan|
    \item \verb|suv|
    \item \verb|pickup_truck|
    \item \verb|van|
    \item \verb|bus|
    \item \verb|truck|
    \item \verb|two_wheeler|
    \item \verb|unknown_vehicle|
\end{itemize}

These labels were chosen because they represent vehicle groupings that are reasonably 
aligned with broader transportation and emissions categories while still remaining 
visually identifiable in 352×240 traffic-camera images. In particular, sedans, coupes,
and hatchbacks were all labeled as \verb|Sedan|, while sport utility vehicles and crossovers 
were both labeled as \verb|SUV|. This consolidation was intentional. Federal transportation 
and emissions frameworks generally operate on broader vehicle classes rather than 
highly specific consumer body styles. For example, the FHWA Traffic Monitoring 
Guide groups sedans and coupes within the passenger-car category, while EPA and 
DOE materials similarly distinguish broad classes such as passenger cars, passenger 
trucks, buses, trucks, and motorcycles for transportation and emissions analysis 
\cite{FHWA_traffic_2014} \cite{USEPA_def_truck_2019} \cite{USDOE_AFDC}.

This simplification was also necessary because the dataset consists of low-resolution 
CCTV images, where fine-grained distinctions between similar passenger body styles 
are often visually unreliable. In many views, a coupe, sedan, and hatchback differ 
primarily in roofline or rear profile, while a crossover and SUV may differ only subtly 
in ride height or body proportions. With the low-resolution images, these features are 
often obscured by distance, motion blur, occlusion, and unfavorable camera angle. To 
improve consistency when distinguishing visually similar passenger vehicles, labeling 
decisions were guided by a small set of practical visual heuristics. In side or angled 
views, SUVs were generally identified by their taller cabin profile, greater apparent 
ride height, and larger overall body proportions relative to nearby sedans. Sedans were 
typically lower to the ground, with a thinner body profile and a more downward-sloping 
rear.

These cues were not treated as absolute rules, but rather as 
consistent guidelines used to reduce labeling variability across ambiguous cases. 
Grouping visually similar body styles into broader categories therefore reduced 
annotation ambiguity and helped limit label noise, which is especially important 
in a manually labeled dataset where forcing uncertain distinctions can degrade 
downstream model performance.


At the same time, the annotation classes were intentionally made somewhat more 
descriptive than the final class structure used in the present training pipeline. 
Although \verb|suv| and \verb|pickup_truck| are ultimately grouped into the MOVES 
passenger-truck category during model training and downstream emissions mapping, 
they were annotated separately in the raw dataset to preserve semantically useful 
detail at the labeling stage. This decision was made primarily for annotation 
clarity, interpretability, and dataset flexibility. In practice, SUVs and pickup 
trucks are often recognizable as distinct vehicle forms in imagery even if they 
are later collapsed into the same broader analytical category. Retaining these 
labels in the annotations therefore made the dataset easier to review and also 
allows it to be reused for future machine-learning tasks that may require a more 
detailed passenger-vehicle taxonomy. This later aggregation is still consistent 
with EPA guidance, which places many SUVs and similar vehicles within broader 
light-duty truck or passenger-truck categories for emissions analysis 
\cite{USEPA_def_truck_2019}, \cite{USEPA_road_pop_2020}.

A similar logic applies to \verb|bus| and \verb|truck|, but in the opposite direction. 
In MOVES, buses are divided into categories such as other bus, transit bus, and 
school bus, while trucks are separated into source types such as light commercial 
truck, refuse truck, single-unit short-haul truck, single-unit long-haul truck, 
combination short-haul truck, and combination long-haul truck 
\cite{USDOE_AFDC}, \cite{USEPA_road_pop_2020}. However, these distinctions are not 
based on vehicle appearance alone. EPA documentation defines bus categories partly 
by ownership and service type. For example, transit buses are associated with 
public transit operations, school buses with K--12 student transport, and other 
buses with non-transit, non-school use \cite{USEPA_road_pop_2020}. Likewise, truck 
source types are differentiated partly by vehicle configuration, axle arrangement, 
haul type, and primary distance of operation rather than only visual silhouette 
\cite{USEPA_moves3_2020}.

In low-resolution 352$\times$240 CCTV imagery, many of these distinctions are 
either not visible or not reliably inferable from a single frame. A shuttle bus 
operated by a private company and a visually similar shuttle operated by a public 
transit agency may appear nearly identical in the image while belonging to different 
MOVES source types. Similarly, distinguishing a single-unit short-haul truck from 
a single-unit long-haul truck, or a refuse truck from another heavy truck under 
occlusion, blur, or poor viewing angle, may not be consistently possible from image 
evidence alone \cite{USEPA_road_pop_2020}, \cite{USEPA_moves3_2020}. For this reason, 
all buses were labeled under a single \verb|bus| class and all heavier non-pickup 
trucks under a single \verb|truck| class.

One exception was the addition of the \verb|is_school| attribute for buses. Unlike 
other MOVES bus subcategories, school buses are often visually distinctive because 
of their characteristic body shape and highly recognizable yellow color. Rather than 
creating a separate top-level \verb|school_bus| class, this subtype was recorded as 
an attribute so that the dataset could preserve potentially useful semantic 
information without expanding the main detection taxonomy. This attribute-based 
approach maintains annotation consistency while still allowing future analyses to 
identify school buses when that visual distinction can be made with reasonable 
confidence.

Overall, this design prioritizes visually reliable labeling while preserving useful 
semantic detail where possible. Classes were kept separate when the distinction was 
clear and potentially useful for future reuse of the dataset, as in the case of 
\verb|suv| and \verb|pickup_truck|, but were collapsed when finer distinctions 
depended primarily on operational or contextual information that could not be 
reliably inferred from low-resolution imagery, as in the case of most MOVES bus and 
truck subcategories. The result is an annotation scheme that is intentionally 
image-driven for model development, even though the later emissions analysis uses a 
more analytically driven mapping to MOVES source types 
\cite{USEPA_def_truck_2019}, \cite{USEPA_road_pop_2020}, \cite{USEPA_moves3_2020}.

\subsubsection{Attributes}

Several classes were assigned boolean attributes to capture subcategories that may 
be useful for later analysis:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \verb|sedan|: \verb|is_taxi|, \verb|is_police| 
    \item \verb|suv|: \verb|is_taxi|, \verb|is_police|
    \item \verb|van|: \verb|is_taxi|, \verb|is_minivan| 
    \item \verb|truck|: \verb|is_delivery|, \verb|is_firetruck|, \verb|is_ambulance|
    \item \verb|bus|: \verb|is_school|
\end{itemize}

No additional attributes were assigned to \verb|pickup_truck|, \verb|two_wheeler|, 
or \verb|unknown_vehicle|.

This attribute-based design was used to make the dataset more flexible without 
unnecessarily expanding the number of top-level detection classes. In CVAT, 
attributes function as additional object properties that can enrich labels
without introducing a separate object category, making them well suited for 
subtypes that may be analytically meaningful but are not always reliable as 
standalone detection classes in low-resolution imagery 
\cite{CVAT_label_guide} \cite{CVAT_attributes}.

    \centering
    \includegraphics[height=5cm]{figures/dataset/3_4_2_minivan_attribute.png}
    \caption{Example of an attribute given to a vehicle in CVAT. In this case, the

\FloatBarrier

For this reason, the primary classes were reserved for visually robust vehicle 
groups, while attributes were used to preserve finer-grained semantic information 
when it could be identified with reasonable confidence. For example, a minivan 
may appear too visually similar to other vans or SUVs to justify a separate top-level 
class in 352×240 traffic-camera images, yet it may still be useful to record 
through an attribute. Similarly, taxis, police vehicles, ambulances, fire trucks, 
delivery trucks, and city vehicles may be relevant for downstream policy, 
fleet-composition, or municipal-operations analyses, even if they are not modeled
as separate classes in the present detection pipeline.

This design also improves the long-term utility of the dataset. By storing these 
subtypes as attributes rather than discarding them entirely, the dataset remains
reusable for other machine-learning or transportation-analysis tasks, including 
subclass filtering, targeted error analysis, or later regrouping into alternative
classes. At the same time, keeping visually ambiguous subtypes out of the main 
class list helps reduce label noise, which is important because noisy or inconsistent 
annotations can degrade model training and evaluation quality \cite{CVAT_annotation_quality}.

\subsubsection{Unknown Vehicle Class}
\label{sec:unk-vehicle-class}

An additional class, \verb|unknown_vehicle|, was introduced to handle ambiguous 
cases where a vehicle was clearly present but could not be labeled confidently as
one of the primary types. In practice, this most often occurred when the low image
quality, occlusion, motion blur, extreme viewing angle, or vehicle distance made 
it difficult to distinguish between visually similar classes, especially sedans and
SUVs. See Figure~\ref{fig:unknown-vehicles} for an example.

Including the \verb|unknown_vehicle| class served two purposes. First, it prevented
uncertain objects from being forced into incorrect labels, which would introduce 
label noise into the training data. Second, it allowed the annotation process to
remain consistent as vehicles were still marked as present in the scene, but without 
overstating certainty about their exact type. This is especially important in 
low-resolution urban traffic imagery, where fine-grained body-style distinctions 
are not always visually reliable.

Because the goal of the dataset was not simply to maximize the number of labeled 
vehicles, but to create a high-quality training set for downstream emissions-oriented
classification, preserving uncertainty explicitly rather than introducing mislabeled
images was preferred.

\subsubsection{Annotation Procedure}

Each image was manually reviewed in CVAT and every visible vehicle of interest was 
annotated with a rectangular bounding box. The assigned box was drawn tightly 
around the visible extent of the vehicle body. After drawing the bounding box, the
most appropriate class label was selected. When applicable, additional attributes 
were checked to indicate more specific vehicle roles or subtypes.

This annotation procedure was repeated across all 1,200 images in the dataset. 
Since the images were drawn from multiple camera locations and viewing angles, the
labeling process captured vehicles under a wide range of perspectives, including 
front-facing, rear-facing, side-facing, and mixed-angle views. Vehicles also appeared
under different traffic densities, illumination conditions, and levels of partial
occlusion. As a result, the final annotated dataset reflects a realistic urban 
traffic-camera detection environment rather than a controlled benchmark setting.

\subsection{Dataset Splits}
\label{sec:data-splits}

After annotation, the full dataset was partitioned into training, validation, and
test subsets using an 80/10/10 split at the image level. To reduce the risk that
one subset would be dominated by only a few camera viewpoints, the split was 
constructed so that images from each camera location were evenly distributed across the 
training, validation, and test sets. The training set was used to fit the model 
parameters, the validation set was used for model selection and hyperparameter 
tuning, and the test set was reserved for final evaluation.  The final split 
contained 960 training images, 120 validation images, and 120 test images, 
corresponding to the 1,200 total annotated images in the dataset.
