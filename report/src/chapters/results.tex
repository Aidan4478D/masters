\section{Experimentation and Results}

Vehicle detection experiments in this study were centered on a 6-class taxonomy 
designed to support emissions-oriented analysis. Several preliminary configurations 
were explored during early development, but those runs were primarily used to 
understand the difficulty of the task and refine the problem formulation. Thus,
the results presented in this section focus on experiments conducted under the 
final 6-class setting. 

First, YOLO-based experiments are presented, including comparisons across model
variants and refinements through data-centric strategies such as hard negative 
mining and dataset refinement. Other detectors like DETR-based models and Faster
R-CNN were examined to compare architectures within the project’s context. 

After identifying the strongest detector, additional experiments evaluate its 
robustness through threshold and NMS sensitivity analysis, cross-camera generalization,
and agreement between predicted vehicle counts and reference ATR or manual counts. 
Together, these experiments exhibit the practical suitability of the final system 
for traffic-monitoring applications.

\subsection{MOVES-based Taxonomy}

For the experiments reported in this chapter, model training and evaluation were 
conducted using a final 6-class taxonomy: 
\verb|passenger_car|, \verb|passenger_truck|, \verb|light_commerical_vehicle|, 
\verb|bus|, \verb|truck|, and \verb|motorcycle|. 
This label set was selected to provide a practical balance between compatibility
with downstream MOVES-based emissions analysis and what could be distinguished 
reliably from low-resolution traffic-camera imagery. Rather than reproducing the
full MOVES source-type framework, the taxonomy preserves broad categories relevant 
to emissions modeling while reducing visual distinctions that are difficult to 
learn consistently.

To achieve this balance, fine-grained vehicle labels were grouped accordingly. 
Sedan instances were mapped to \verb|passenger_car|, while \verb|SUV| instances, 
\verb|pickup_truck| instances, and \verb|van| instances with the attribute \verb|is_minivan| 
instances were grouped into \verb|passenger_truck|. The EPA notes that it is 
difficult to visually determine whether a vehicle should be treated as a 
passenger car or a light-duty truck, but classifies many SUVs and minivans as 
light-duty trucks rather than passenger cars \cite{USEPA_def_truck_2019}. 

The remaining van instances were mapped to \verb|light_commercial_vehicle|. 
This separation is useful from a computer-vision standpoint, as commercial vans 
tend to have more consistent shapes and usage patterns than SUVs and pickup trucks.
It also aligns with MOVES technical guidance, which treats passenger trucks and 
light commercial vehicles as separate source types \cite{USEPA_moves3_2020}. 
Additionally, since the original \verb|two_wheeler| class only contained 
motorcycles, it was remapped directly to \verb|motorcycle|.

Finally, \verb|bus| and \verb|truck| were preserved as independent classes because they are 
operationally different, visually distinct, and treated as separate categories 
within MOVES. Within the bus category, however, school buses were not modeled 
as a separate detector class, even though MOVES distinguishes other buses, 
transit buses, and school buses as separate source types. Instead, school buses
were retained as an annotation attribute and grouped into the broader bus class. 
This is because the primary objective of this study is to identify the broad 
vehicle categories most relevant to downstream emissions-oriented analysis rather
than to infer every possible subtype within each category. As such, the bus 
attribute, \verb|is_school|, was preserved as useful annotation metadata and a potential
avenue for future refinement, but it was not treated as essential to the core 
detector taxonomy. The final mapping between the dataset taxonomy and the MOVES 
taxonomy is shown below in Figure~\ref{fig:moves-taxonomy}.

\begin{figure}[!htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[height=7cm]{figures/results/5_1_class_taxonomy.png}
    \caption{Final MOVES-based class mapping used in this study. Dataset classes 
         on the left were mapped to the MOVES-aligned detector classes on the 
         right. Arrows of the same color indicate the corresponding class 
         mapping, with van instances labeled \texttt{is\_minivan} grouped into 
         \texttt{passenger\_truck} and the remaining van instances mapped to 
         \texttt{light\_commercial\_truck}.}
    \label{fig:moves-taxonomy}
\end{figure}

This MOVES-based taxonomy was used for the remainder of the study as the common 
label mapping for the dataset and for all subsequent training, testing, and evaluation.

\subsection{YOLO-Based Detector Development and Results}

\subsubsection{Baseline YOLO Experiments}

The first YOLO experiments under the MOVES-based taxonomy were conducted by 
fine-tuning baseline YOLO models on the training split and validating them on 
the validation split defined in Section \ref{sec:data-splits}. Each model was 
trained to predict the six final vehicle classes --- passenger car, passenger truck, 
light commercial truck, bus, truck, and motorcycle --- directly from traffic-camera 
images. The purpose of this stage was to assess whether YOLO could learn the 
6-class formulation effectively and provide a strong starting point for later 
refinement.

To evaluate CNN-based one-stage detectors in a controlled and comparable way, 
this study focused on YOLOv8, YOLO11, and YOLO26, which represent three distinct 
generations of the Ultralytics YOLO family. YOLOv8 (2023) was selected as a strong and 
widely adopted baseline, since it introduced the anchor-free split head and was 
designed as a general-purpose real-time detector with a favorable speed-accuracy 
tradeoff \cite{yolov8_ultralytics} \cite{ultralytics_yolov8_vs_yolov11}. 
YOLO11 (2024) was included because it is a newer Ultralytics detector that builds 
on earlier YOLO models with improved feature extraction, training efficiency, and 
parameter efficiency, making it a natural candidate for higher accuracy under 
the same general framework \cite{yolo11_ultralytics} \cite{ultralytics_yolov8_vs_yolov11}. 
As of April 2026, YOLO26 is Ultralytics' most recent YOLO model, tested as an 
edge-oriented variant designed for simpler deployment, with end-to-end NMS-free 
inference and targeted improvements for efficiency and small-object recognition 
\cite{yolo26_ultralytics} \cite{ultralytics_yolov8_vs_yolov11}. 
Evaluating these three models therefore allowed comparison across a 
consistent detector family while covering a useful range of design priorities: 
a mature baseline (YOLOv8), a newer accuracy-focused refinement (YOLO11), and a
deployment-oriented architecture with simplified inference (YOLO26). This made 
them well suited for the present task, which required not only accurate vehicle 
detection but also practical applicability to traffic-monitoring and counting 
workflows. To enable a more controlled comparison across YOLO generations, all 
experiments in this subsection used the small (S) variant, which provided a 
consistent mid-scale tradeoff between model capacity, compute, and efficiency 
\cite{ultralytics_coco}.

To establish baseline performance, each YOLO variant was fine-tuned under the 
same general training configuration. All baseline models were trained for 50 
epochs with a batch size of 8, since the validation mAP curves indicated that 
performance had largely plateaued by that point. Unless otherwise noted, the 
same default augmentation pipeline was used across all YOLO variants so that 
observed differences would primarily reflect architectural differences rather 
than changes in training conditions. During training and evaluation, images 
were resized to $768 \times 1280$, and bounding-box annotations were converted from 
the original CVAT XML format into normalized YOLO label files under the final 
6-class mapping. Instances labeled \verb|unknown_vehicle| were not treated as 
a supervised detector class and were instead handled according to the ignore-region
procedure described in Section \ref{sec:unknown-vehicles}.

As shown in Table~\ref{tab:yolo-baseline-results} and Figure~\ref{fig:baseline-yolo-charts}, 
all three baseline YOLO variants converged within 50 epochs on the validation set 
under the MOVES-based taxonomy. YOLOv8s achieved an AP50 of 0.750 and a mAP of 0.606,
while YOLOv11s improved this to an AP50 of 0.804 and a mAP of 0.615. YOLOv26s 
produced the highest baseline mAP at 0.632, while remaining close to YOLOv11s 
in AP50 with a value of 0.802. Since YOLOv11s and YOLOv26s represented the strongest 
baseline models in terms of AP50 and mAP, respectively, both were carried forward 
into the subsequent refinement experiments. The next stage of experiments investigated
whether these baseline results could be further improved through data-centric 
refinement strategies.

\subsubsection{Data-Centric Refinements}

\textbf{Dataset Refinement}

After establishing the baseline YOLO results, the next stage of experimentation 
focused on data-centric refinement guided by manual inspection of model errors. 
Rather than changing the detector architecture, this stage used the baseline 
confusion patterns to identify which types of examples required closer review. 
Particular attention was given to the dominant confusion between passenger car 
and passenger truck, as well as to vehicles that appeared visually ambiguous in 
the original imagery. Model predictions that did not match the validation set 
label were not used to relabel the dataset automatically, but instead served to 
flag candidate examples for manual review. The validation confusion matrices 
before dataset refinement for the baseline YOLOv11s and YOLOv26s models are 
shown in Figure~\ref{fig:val-cm-pre}. These predictions followed the same ignore-aware
evaluation outlined in Section~\ref{sec:unknown-vehicles}.

\textbf{Hard Negative Mining}

The primary refinement strategy that followed was hard negative mining. As indicated
by the post-refinement validation confusion matrices in Figure~X, the dominant 
remaining error for both baseline models was the confusion between passenger cars 
and passenger trucks. To target this directly, the training set was scanned using
the baseline YOLOv11s model for YOLOv11s refinement and the baseline YOLOv26s 
model for YOLOv26s refinement to identify images in which a ground-truth passenger
car was predicted as a passenger truck, or a ground-truth passenger truck was 
predicted as a passenger car. In this mining step, each ground-truth passenger 
car or passenger truck was matched to its highest-overlap prediction, and an 
image was flagged when that match exceeded an IoU threshold of 0.50 but was 
assigned to the opposite class. Figure~\ref{fig:hard-negative-example} shows an 
example of a hard negative identified by this procedure, in which vehicles were 
confused between the passenger car and passenger truck classes.

A sweep was performed over the mining confidence threshold and the number of difficult 
images retained, and the strongest configuration used a confidence threshold of 
0.05 with the top 100 difficult images added back into the training set. This 
targeted refinement produced the best-performing detector in the study. Compared
with the baseline YOLOv11s experiment, training a new YOLOv11s model on the 
original training set augmented with the mined hard examples improved validation
performance from AP50 = 0.804 to 0.857 and from mAP = 0.615 to 0.661. This was 
the strongest overall result obtained among the YOLO experiments.

Additional data-centric experiments were also explored through selective oversampling
of weaker classes, including \verb|light_commercial_truck|, \verb|truck|, and \verb|motorcycle|. 
However, these broader class-specific duplication strategies did not improve 
upon the best hard-negative-mined configuration. A local grid search was also 
performed around the baseline hyperparameter settings to determine whether 
further gains could be obtained through optimization alone, but none of the 
tested configurations improved upon the best hard-negative-mined YOLOv11s model 
in either AP50 or mAP. Comparable hard-negative experiments were also conducted 
using YOLOv26s, with additional variation in the mining confidence threshold 
and the number of selected images. Although these runs produced small improvements,
none exceeded the best refined YOLOv11s result. Further refinement runs were 
also carried out by initializing training from previously trained baseline and 
refined YOLOv11s and YOLOv26s checkpoints rather than from the standard pretrained 
Ultralytics weights. In these cases, training generally plateaued at lower AP50 
and mAP values, even when hyperparameters were varied, indicating that continued
fine-tuning from existing checkpoints was less effective than retraining a new 
model on the augmented dataset. Selected strong-performing YOLO results across 
the YOLOv11s and YOLOv26s backbones, including baseline, mined, oversampled, 
hyperparameter-search, and continued fine-tuning configurations, are summarized 
in Table~\ref{tab:yolo-refinement-summary}.

\subsubsection{Final YOLO Model Selection and Test-Set Evaluation}

Based on the baseline comparisons and subsequent data-centric refinement experiments, 
the final detector selected for this study was YOLOv11s, saved as checkpoint 
\verb|vehicle_6c_v11s11|. For readability, this checkpoint is referred to

\subsection{Comparison with Alternative Detection Architectures}

\subsubsection{Faster R-CNN}

Like the YOLO experiments, Faster R-CNN experiments were conducted under the 
MOVES-based taxonomy. For implementation, torchvision Faster R-CNN models with 
ResNet-50 feature pyramid backbones were used, including both the standard 
\verb|fasterrcnn_resnet50_fpn| model and the newer \verb|fasterrcnn_resnet50_fpn_v2|
variant provided in torchvision. The \verb|fasterrcnn_resnet50_fpn_v2| model is 
described in the torchvision documentation as an improved Faster R-CNN model with 
a ResNet-50-FPN backbone \cite{torchvision2016}.

For the Faster R-CNN experiments, the detector head was adapted to the MOVES-based
taxonomy by replacing the default box predictor with a new predictor for six 
foreground classes plus background. The training pipeline used the same train/validation
split as the other detectors. A small-object-oriented anchor generator was used 
with anchor sizes of 8, 16, 32, 64, and 128 pixels across the feature pyramid, 
and like the YOLO experiments, images were resized to $768 \times 1280$. Training used 
AdamW with a learning rate of $2\times10^{-4}$, weight decay of $10^{-4}$, and 
a cosine annealing learning-rate schedule over 50 epochs. Light augmentation was 
applied during training, consisting of random horizontal flipping and modest 
color jitter in the best runs. Batch size was set to 4 for training and 2 for 
validation. As in the YOLO and transformer experiments, \verb|unknown_vehicle| 
instances were masked during training and treated as ignore regions during evaluation, 
following the same ignore-aware procedure outlined in Section~\ref{sec:unknown-vehicles}.

A light targeted grid search was conducted for the Faster R-CNN experiments to 
evaluate a small number of influential training choices without the cost of a 
full exhaustive sweep. The search varied four factors: backbone variant 
(\verb|fasterrcnn_resnet50_fpn_v2| versus \verb|fasterrcnn_resnet50_fpn|), \
learning rate ($1\times10^{-4}$ and $2\times10^{-4}$), augmentation strategy 
(random horizontal flipping only versus horizontal flipping with modest color 
jitter), and the number of trainable backbone layers (3 versus 5). Weight decay
was held fixed at $1\times10^{-4}$, and each run was trained for 50 epochs under
the same general pipeline. Table~\ref{tab:faster-rcnn-results} summarizes the 
strongest Faster R-CNN results discussed in this section, while Appendix~\ref{app:A} 
reports the full set of grid-search results.

\subsubsection{Detection Transformer (DETR)}

Three transformer-based detectors were evaluated under the MOVES-based taxonomy: 
SenseTime Deformable DETR, Peking University RT-DETR, and Peking University RT-DETRv2. 
These models were selected to provide a comparison across recent DETR-style detector 
designs within a common implementation framework. Deformable DETR served as an 
established multi-scale transformer baseline, while RT-DETR and RT-DETRv2 represented 
more efficiency-oriented DETR variants intended to improve the speed-accuracy tradeoff 
\cite{detr2020,deformabledetr2020,rtdetr2024,rtdetrv22024}.

For these experiments, the detector backbone was kept frozen for the first 10 epochs 
and then unfrozen for the remainder of training, with the full model trained for 50 
epochs using AdamW and a weight decay of $10^{-4}$. A cosine learning-rate schedule
was used, and learning rates were selected through iterative tuning rather than 
exhaustive search, since a single training run required several hours. In practice, 
parameter settings were chosen by monitoring whether validation performance remained 
stable and continued improving over training, while avoiding configurations that caused
the detector to stall or degrade early. The best learning rates for Deformable DETR 
and RT-DETR are summarized in Table~X.

Training augmentation was ultimately kept light, consisting primarily of horizontal
flipping and modest brightness/contrast perturbations. This decision was motivated 
by earlier experiments using a heavier Albumentations pipeline, which suggested
that transformer-based detectors were sensitive to stronger image transformations
in this low-resolution traffic-camera setting. In this section, heavier Albumentations 
pipeline refers to the augmentation setup used in the first Deformable DETR 
experiment, and lighter Albumentations pipeline refers to the simplified setup used
in the subsequent experiments. The exact transform definitions are listed in Appendix~\ref{app:B}.
As in the YOLO and R-CNN experiments, \verb|unknown_vehicle| instances were masked 
during training, and treated as ignore regions during evaluation.

Although RT-DETRv2 was the strongest transformer-based model evaluated, its
